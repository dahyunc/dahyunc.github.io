Teaming Up Across Political Divides: Evidence from Climate Regulations

(Previously titled "Teaming up with the Enemy: Firms and the Information Environment of Climate Regulations.")

[ | paper | ]
Why do interest groups with contrasting interests and policy goals work together? I present a theory of collaborative policy production and show that interest groups can achieve higher policy gains through collaboration, even though their ideal policy goals may diverge significantly. To test theoretical results, I introduce original measurement strategies that reveal systematic patterns in which firms and environmental groups invest in joint efforts to improve fine-grained details of policy to achieve greenhouse gas emissions targets. The analysis, using public comments spanning 2010-2020, demonstrates that comments written jointly by environmental groups and firms contain more information that can contribute to the quality of policy implementation than individual efforts alone, despite compromises on policy preferences. These findings highlight the hidden dynamics of regulatory politics, wherein divergent political goals are reconciled for high-quality policy implementation.

    Learning from Noise: Applying Sample Complexity for Social Science Research

    (with Perry Carter)

    [ | paper | ]
    While statistical learning bridges the gap between theoretical concepts and complex empirical realities, the question of what constitutes ``good enough" data for social scientists remains understudied. In this article, we introduce the Probably Approximately Correct model and present sample complexity bounds, which take advantage of researcher-specified estimates of labeling error to guarantee the sample size required for a minimum level of accuracy. We develop a simulation-based approach to demonstrate its feasibility and provide the scR R package, offering a computationally efficient way to implement the proposed methods. We aim to improve standard practice by providing a general-purpose tool to validate the quality of measures when fuzzy measurement boundaries make generating data with ground-truth labels infeasible.

    • Diminishing Regulatory Capacity and Corporate Political Disengagement: Evidence from State-Level Workforce Shocks

    (with Kyuwon Lee) [ | ]
    Although there are public concerns about the declining capacity of regulatory agencies and its impact on regulatory outcomes, such decline could also lead regulated firms to disengage themselves from politics. We examine whether and how firms reduce their campaign contributions in response to decreases in state-level regulatory capacity. To do so, we collect original datasets on the workforce size of U.S. state environmental agencies and leverage variations in workforce shocks that arise from the gap between actual and appropriated workforce sizes. Our analysis reveals that state environmental agencies' workforce shocks decrease firms' donations to state legislators, particularly to those in the majority party and the Democratic party, but do not affect firms' contributions to their ideological allies. We also find that existing state-level restrictions on corporate donations do not moderate firms' political responsiveness. Overall, this article provides a nuanced picture of how diminishing regulatory capacity could shape corporate political activities.

    How Do Bureaucrats Learn in Absence of Autonomous Sources of Expertise?

    [ | ]
    A large body of literature points to bureaucratic expertise as an important factor in policy outcomes, while the question of how bureaucrats obtain policy-relevant information is relatively understudied. I demonstrate that in a policy environment where policy-specific expertise is concentrated in firms, the bureaucratic acquisition of expertise is contingent upon the political influence of private interests. Using an original dataset revealing the bureaucratic monitoring behavior of the Greenhouse Gas Reporting Program with a new measure of partisan alignment between firms and bureaucracy, I examine the practices of how bureaucrats collect information from regulated entities. This paper highlights the underlying, but underappreciated, sources of bureaucratic expertise when establishing an autonomous source of information is impossible.

    • Scientification of Politics? Interest Group Influence on Bureaucratic Expertise in Climate Change

    [ | ]
    To what extent do interest groups influence the processes through which bureaucrats obtain scientific and policy-relevant information? While science has been considered the primary source of expertise that helps regulators improve policy outcomes, the use of scientific information is inevitably intertwined with political reasoning. To explore this dynamic, I construct an original dataset that reveals the Environmental Protection Agency's internal decision-making process and examine how the proximity of each scientific study's funders to the agency affects the study's chances of being cited by bureaucrats in policy documents. My analysis provides evidence that bureaucrats are better informed about the quality of scientific studies but are also biased in their favor. Overall, the effect of quality appears to outweigh the bias introduced by the proximity between bureaucrats and the interest groups that fund scientific studies. This paper not only provides suggestive evidence on the roles of expertise and bias in the policymaking process but also contributes to our understanding of interest group influence on bureaucratic expertise.
